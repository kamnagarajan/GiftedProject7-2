Glacial effects

Glaciers have had a lot of effects on earth in the past. One of the more obvious examples would be changes in sea level. During the last age, sea level was 400 meters below now.

Isostatic Rebound

Isostatic rebound is another effect glaciers have on earth. When huge glaciers form, they push down the crust beneath them. That causes the crust around them to start rising up then go back sown again. Of course this takes place on a very large time scale, and there are some examples of this right now. The area around Greenland is still rising due to the weight of Ice Sheet. If this is hard to understand, one thing you could compare it to is when you push down on a soft surface such as a pillow. When you push down on one spot the area surround it comes up, and when you lift your finger away the area goes back to how it was before. This is basically what isostatic rebound is, but over a very long time scale.

Runaway Albedo Effect

The runaway albedo effect is also something that would have a great impact on us. Albedo is the amount of light a surface reflects back. To start with, ice has an albedo of 55-80% while ocean water typically has an albedo of 12-15%. If a surface has an albedo of 100%, then it reflects all of the light, but if it has an albedo of 0%, then it reflects nothing back. This means that glaciers are better than ocean water at helping reduce the temperature rise. Since earth is already warming, this means that we’re losing the amount of reflection we have, which leads to earth getting warmer and losing more glaciers. You can see where they get “runaway” part from, glaciers stat melting faster and faster. This also works the other way, if the earth starts cooling, ice sheets reflect even more heat into space which leads to more ice sheets forming.
