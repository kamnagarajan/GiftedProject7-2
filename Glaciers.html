Glacials landforms

Glacial landforms are mainly classified by how they are formed. There are two main types, erosional and depositional. Then, there is also glacial fluvial. Erosional features are what's left of that part of a glacier after erosion occurs. Depositional features are formed after part of a glacier has been taken of.

Erosional Features

These types of features are made when a other forces remove ice from a part of a glacier, or when a glacier erodes its surroundings. One example of this would be a U-valley. U-valleys are made after a glacier passes through a valley.

A glacier can remove mass from its surroundings in multiple ways. One way is Abrasion. When a glacier loosens the rocks of its surroundings, and then carries the rocks with it, that is called plucking. If two glaciers pass by each other on a flat valley, a horn would form in between them. A horn is an example of an erosional glacial feature.

Another example of an erosional feature would be a cirque. A cirque is a depressed bowl shaped valley. For a cirque to form, a glacier would form in a lake, then after a bowl shaped depression forms and the glacier melts, what it’s left behind would be a cirque. The glacier itself can form a cirque on a flat valley from the abrasion occurring on the bottom between the ground and itself.

Depositional features

Depositional features are what’s left over from the glacier after it retreats. One of the biggest depositional features are moraines. Moraines are left over rock from the rock the glacier picked up. They are classified by their location on the glacier.

Erratics are also a type of depositional feature. When a glacier picks up rocks, it also carries them till a glacier melts. There are times when a a glacier picks up a huge rock then carries it thousands of miles away from its original habitat, in that case it would be called an erratic. The rock is usually way diffferent from its surroundings.

Drumlins are another type of despositional feature. After a glacier passes over some land, there’s a lot of feature it leaves behind, one of them being drumlins. A drum line is an elongated sloped hill. One side of the glacier is steep while the other has a gentle slope. From this, we can usually tell which direction the glacier flowed.

Glaciers are usually categorized by how they are formed, so once you know a glaciers categorization, you would be able to tell how it’s been formed.
