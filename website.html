What are glaciers ?

Glaciers are a slowly moving mass or river of ice formed by the accumulation and compaction of snow on mountains or near the poles.

Formation

When snow falls, it's later recrystallized and becomes firn. Firn is snow except more compacted with less space bubbles. For a glacier to be formed, the amount of snowfall has to be bigger than the amount now melted away. To be considered a glacier, it needs to start moving, and for that to happen its most likely going to be 100 ft. thicker or more. The minimum size for a glacier is about 25 acres or 100,000 miles ^2.

Flow

After a glacier starts moving, there’s the entire thing with how fast it flow and it’s zones. There mainly three zones that seperate the parts of a glacier that flow the way they do. The first one is the accumulation zone. This is the area where the amount of snowfalls is greater than the amount of snow that melts in the summer. Then there’s the equilibrium line. This is the part where the amount of snowfall and the amount of snow melthing away in the summer are equal. After that, there’s the ablation zone where the amount of ice thats melted away is greater than the amount of snowfall.

Types of Glaciers

There are mainly two categories of glacier types that exist; Continental and Alpine glaciers. Continental glaciers are dome shaped and cover a lot of area while Alpine glaciers are usually smaller. Go to Glacial Landforms to learn more.
